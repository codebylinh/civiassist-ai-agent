## Community Safety and Resident Relations

### Noise Ordinances
Most jurisdictions restrict construction noise to weekdays 7AM–6PM and Saturdays 8AM–5PM.
Night work (after 9PM) typically requires a special permit. Exceeding permitted hours is a
code violation — residents have the right to contact the city inspector. Typical decibel limits
near residential areas: 75 dBA at the property line during daytime.

### Dust Control Requirements
Active construction must implement dust control: water suppression on exposed soil, covering
stockpiles, wheel wash stations at site exits, street sweeping of mud/debris. Failure to
control dust affecting neighboring properties can trigger complaints to the local air quality
management district (AQMD) and may result in fines or stop-work orders.

### Excavation Near Occupied Buildings
Excavation within 50 ft of an occupied structure requires a preconstruction survey (photos,
crack mapping). Vibration monitoring may be required. If new cracks appear in neighboring
structures during excavation, work must stop and a structural engineer must assess whether
the damage is construction-related. This is a legal and insurance matter.

### Traffic Management and Road Closures
Any lane or road closure requires advance notice to the public (typically 48–72 hours for
planned closures, immediate notification for emergencies). A Traffic Management Plan (TMP)
approved by the local authority is required. Failure to provide notice or proper signage
is a liability risk and ordinance violation.

### Sidewalk and Pedestrian Access
Construction must maintain safe pedestrian passage at all times, or provide an equivalent
alternate route with proper signage. Closing a sidewalk without a safe detour violates ADA
requirements and pedestrian safety ordinances. A pedestrian protection plan is required for
overhead work near public walkways (scaffolding, netting, covered walkways).

### How to Handle an Emergency Complaint
1. Do not dismiss or minimize — treat every emergency complaint as real until proven otherwise
2. If life safety is at risk: call 911 first, then notify the site superintendent
3. Stop work in the affected area immediately
4. Document: photos, time, who was notified, what action was taken
5. Notify the project manager and owner within 1 hour
6. File an incident report within 24 hours
7. Do not resume work in the affected area without sign-off from the project engineer

### Proactive Community Communication Best Practices
- Notify residents at least 48 hours before any unusually noisy, dusty, or disruptive work
- Post a site notice board with: project name, contractor, 24-hour contact number, expected completion
- Respond to every complaint within 24 hours (high/emergency: within 1 hour)
- After resolving a complaint, follow up with the resident to confirm the issue is fixed
- Monthly community updates for long-duration projects (>3 months)

## Construction Project Phases
Typical phases: Pre-Design → Schematic Design → Design Development → Construction Documents → Permitting → Bidding/Procurement → Construction (Mobilization → Foundations → Structure → MEP Rough-in → Envelope → Finishes → Commissioning) → Closeout → Warranty Period.

## RFI (Request for Information)
An RFI is a formal written request from contractor to design team for clarification on drawings or specs. Standard response time is 7–14 calendar days. RFIs that affect schedule or cost trigger a potential change order (PCO). All RFIs must be logged, numbered, and tracked to closure.

## Change Orders
A Change Order (CO) modifies the contract scope, schedule, or price. Flow: issue identified → PCO issued by contractor → design team reviews → owner approves → CO executed. No work on changed scope should proceed without an executed CO.

## Submittals
Submittals are documents (shop drawings, product data, samples) submitted by the contractor for design team review. Review stamps: Approved / Approved as Noted / Revise and Resubmit / Rejected. Fabrication should not begin until submittal is approved.

## Site Safety (OSHA)
Key OSHA construction standards: 29 CFR 1926. Critical areas: fall protection (>6 ft), excavation safety (shoring/sloping for >5 ft), scaffold safety, electrical hazard, confined space. Any safety incident must be reported and documented immediately.

## Geotechnical / Soils
Bearing capacity, settlement, liquefaction risk, and groundwater depth drive foundation design. Always verify geotech report before finalizing foundation type. Soil classifications per USCS (Unified Soil Classification System). Expansive soils (high plasticity clays) require special slab and foundation design.

## Drainage and Stormwater
Stormwater management must comply with local MS4 permit requirements. Key metrics: peak flow rate (Q = CiA), detention/retention pond sizing, water quality treatment (first flush). Post-development runoff should not exceed pre-development rates.

## Urban Planning — Zoning
Zoning ordinances govern: use (residential/commercial/industrial/mixed), density (FAR, lot coverage, units/acre), height limits, setbacks (front/rear/side), parking minimums, and design standards. Variances and conditional use permits require public hearing. Non-conforming uses are typically grandfathered but cannot be expanded.

## Environmental Review
In the US, federal projects require NEPA review (Categorical Exclusion / EA / EIS). State equivalents (e.g., CEQA in California). Key impact areas: air quality, noise, traffic, biological resources, cultural resources, hazardous materials.

## Concrete (ACI 318)
Common mix designs: 3,000 psi (residential slabs), 4,000 psi (structural), 5,000 psi+ (high-strength). Cure time: 28 days to design strength; 70% strength at 7 days. Reinforcement cover: 3" for footings, 1.5" for slabs on grade, 2" for beams/columns exposed to weather.

## Steel (AISC)
Common sections: W-shapes (wide flange) for beams/columns, HSS for columns/bracing, angles for connections. ASTM A992 is standard for W-shapes. Connections: bolted (A325/A490) or welded (E70XX electrode typical). Galvanization or paint system required for exposed/exterior steel.

## Roads and Pavement
Flexible pavement (asphalt) design per AASHTO 93 method or MEPDG. Key layers: subgrade → subbase → base → surface. Rigid pavement (concrete) for heavy truck routes. Minimum lane width: 12 ft (arterial), 10 ft (local). Cul-de-sac radius: 50 ft minimum.

## Client Communication Best Practices
- Always give a timeline with any status update
- Convert technical issues to impact on budget, schedule, and risk — clients track these three things
- Never use acronyms without spelling them out first for clients
- If a problem is found, always lead with the solution, not just the problem
- Document all verbal decisions in writing within 24 hours
